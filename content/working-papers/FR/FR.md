##### Abstract

Financial repression lowers the return on government debt and contributes, all
else equal, towards its liquidation. However, its full effect on the debt-to-GDP
ratio hinges on how repression impacts the economy at large because it alters
investment and saving decisions. We develop and estimate a New Keynesian model
with financial repression. Based on U.S. data for the period 1948–1974, we find,
consistent with earlier work, that repression was pervasive but gradually phased out.
A model-based counterfactual shows that GDP would have been 5 percent lower,
and the debt-to-GDP ratio 20 percentage points higher, had repression not been
phased out.
